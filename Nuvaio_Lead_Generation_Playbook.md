# Nuvaio Travel — Lead Generation Playbook
### Bootstrap Strategy for First 10 Corporate Clients

**Situation:** Brand new, zero clients, under $500/month budget, targeting both corporate travel programs and event/group travel organizers, willing to do outbound + inbound.

**Reality check:** At this stage, with this budget, 80% of your leads will come from outbound effort and relationships — not ads, not SEO, not content marketing. That's not a weakness; it's actually how most successful B2B service businesses land their first 10 clients. The website exists to *validate* you after someone hears your name — it's not going to generate cold traffic on its own yet. The strategy below is built around that truth.

---

## Phase 1: Foundation (Weeks 1–2)
*Goal: Get discoverable and credible before you start outreach*

### 1.1 — Fix the Website (Already In Progress)
Implement the changes from the homepage critique and mockup. Priority order:

- Swap the hero headline and image (30 minutes in WordPress)
- Rewrite service descriptions (1 hour)
- Kill the duplicate founding story (15 minutes)
- Add a contact form that captures company name and email (WPForms free tier, 20 minutes)

**Why this matters first:** Every outbound touch you send will drive people to this site. If they land on "Elevate Your Journey" with a beach sunset, you lose them. The site needs to say "corporate travel partner" within 3 seconds.

### 1.2 — Set Up Your LinkedIn Profile as a Sales Page
Your personal LinkedIn is more important than your website right now. Corporate travel managers and event planners will look you up on LinkedIn before they look at your site.

**Headline format:** "Founder, Nuvaio Travel | Corporate Travel Management for Mid-Size Companies & Events"
(Not: "CEO at Nuvaio Travel" — that tells them nothing about what you do for them.)

**About section:** Adapt the hero copy from the mockup. Lead with the problem you solve, not your bio. First line should be something like: "Most companies are stuck between travel platforms that don't flex and agencies that don't care. I started Nuvaio to be the third option."

**Featured section:** Link to your website. If you write any LinkedIn posts (see Phase 2), pin the best one here.

### 1.3 — Google Business Profile
Create a Google Business Profile for Nuvaio Travel. This is free and it's how you show up in "corporate travel agency near me" searches. Fill out every field: services, hours, description, photos. Even without a physical office, you can set it to a service area business.

### 1.4 — Claim Directory Listings (Free)
Register on these — they're free, take 15 minutes each, and help with both SEO and credibility:

- ASTA (American Society of Travel Advisors) — membership costs money, but the directory is worth it if budget allows later
- Yelp Business (free listing)
- BBB (free basic listing)
- Travel Leaders Network or Virtuoso (if you have consortium affiliations)
- Alignable (free B2B networking for local businesses)

## Phase 2: Outbound Engine (Weeks 2–6)
*Goal: Generate your first 5–10 qualified conversations*

This is where your leads come from at this stage. Not ads. Not SEO. Direct outreach.

### 2.1 — Build Your Target List

**Corporate travel targets:**
Use LinkedIn Sales Navigator (free 30-day trial) to find:
- Job titles: Office Manager, Executive Assistant, Director of Operations, VP of Finance, Chief of Staff, Travel Coordinator
- Company size: 50–500 employees
- Industries with heavy travel: consulting, construction, energy, professional services, pharma, tech
- Geography: Start with your metro area for warm intros, then expand

**Event/group travel targets:**
- Meeting planners and event coordinators at associations and nonprofits
- Corporate event teams at mid-size companies
- Conference organizers (search "event planner" or "meeting planner" on LinkedIn)
- DMCs (Destination Management Companies) who need air support
- Search for companies posting event coordinator job listings — they have events coming

**Build a simple spreadsheet:** Name, Title, Company, LinkedIn URL, Email (use Hunter.io free tier or Apollo.io free tier to find emails), Notes.

Aim for 100 names in your first list. That's your pipeline seed.

### 2.2 — LinkedIn Outreach Sequence

**Connection request (keep it short, no pitch):**
> Hi [Name] — I run a corporate travel consultancy and noticed [Company] does a lot of [industry travel / events / conferences]. Would love to connect and learn more about how you're handling travel logistics. — Xavier

**Follow-up message (2–3 days after they accept):**
> Thanks for connecting, [Name]. Quick question — how are you currently managing corporate travel at [Company]? I ask because a lot of mid-size companies I talk to are stuck between platforms like Concur that don't flex and agencies that don't really know their business. If that resonates, I'd be happy to share how we approach it differently at Nuvaio. No pitch — just a 15-minute conversation. Worth your time?

**Key principles:**
- Send 10–15 connection requests per day (stay under LinkedIn's limits)
- Personalize the first line — mention something specific about their company or role
- Never pitch in the connection request
- The goal is a conversation, not a sale
- Follow up once. If no response, move on. Don't be the person who sends 5 messages.

**Email 3 (Day 10) — The breakup email:**

Subject: Should I close the file?

> Hi [Name],

>

> Xavier

**Send volume:** 15–20 new cold emails per day. Use a tool like Instantly.ai ($30/month) or Lemlist (free trial) to automate follow-ups. This is your single highest-ROI spend.

### 2.4 — Event/Group Travel Outreach (Separate Track)

For event planners and conference organizers, the pitch is different:

**LinkedIn message:**
> Hi [Name] — I saw that [Organization] is hosting [Event Name] this year. We specialize in group air travel coordination — air manifests, group bookings, name-change management, and onsite agent support for events from 10 to 500+ travelers. If you're still figuring out the air logistics, I'd love to show you how we make that painless. Would a quick 15-minute call be worth your time?

**Where to find event targets:**
- Search "upcoming conferences 2026 [industry]" and find the organizers
- Check eventbrite.com, cvent.com, and bizzabo.com for upcoming corporate events
- Follow PCMA (Professional Convention Management Association) and MPI (Meeting Professionals International) on LinkedIn — their members are your buyers

## Phase 3: Inbound Foundations (Weeks 3–8)
*Goal: Start building organic visibility so leads eventually come to you*

### 3.1 — LinkedIn Content (Free, 2–3 Posts Per Week)

This is your best free inbound channel. Corporate travel managers and event planners are on LinkedIn. Post about what you know.

**Content themes that work for travel consultancies:**

- **"Here's what I'm seeing" posts:** Share an observation about corporate travel trends. Example: "I reviewed 12 corporate travel programs last month and found the same pattern in almost all of them — nobody's monitoring fare changes after booking. Most companies are leaving 15–20% on the table and don't even know it."

- **"How we handle X" posts:** Walk through your process for a specific service. Example: "When we onboard a new corporate client, the first thing we do is audit their top 10 routes. Here's what we look for..." (Then list 4–5 things in plain language.)

- **"Myth vs. reality" posts:** Challenge a common assumption. Example: "A lot of mid-size companies think they don't have enough travel volume to justify a dedicated travel partner. The breakeven is usually lower than they think. Here's the math..."

- **"Behind the scenes" posts:** Show what happened during a disruption, a group booking, a complex itinerary. (Once you have clients — use anonymized examples.)

**Posting cadence:** 2–3 times per week. Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday mornings get the best engagement on LinkedIn. Keep posts under 1,300 characters. Use a hook in the first two lines.

### 3.2 — Google SEO (Long Game, Start Now)

You won't rank on Google for "corporate travel management" for months — the big players own that. But you can start building authority now with two moves:

**Move 1: Add a blog to your WordPress site.** Write one post per month targeting specific long-tail keywords:

- "How to choose a corporate travel management company"
- "Corporate travel budget planning guide for mid-size companies"
- "Group air travel management for conferences: what to expect"
- "In-house travel coordinator vs. outsourced travel management"
- "How to reduce corporate airfare costs without restricting travelers"

These are low-competition, high-intent keywords that travel managers actually search when evaluating options. Each post should be 800–1,200 words, genuinely helpful, and end with a CTA to book a consultation.

**Move 2: Optimize your homepage** for "corporate travel management" as the primary keyword. The mockup already sets this up — the page title, H1, and first paragraph all target this phrase.

### 3.3 — Email Signature Marketing (Free)

Add this to your personal email signature:

> **Xavier Phillips** | Founder, Nuvaio Travel
> Corporate Travel Management & Group Air Logistics
> Schedule a free consultation: [link to contact page]
> 975-444-0066 | nuvaiotravel.com

Every email you send — personal, professional, replies, forwards — now promotes Nuvaio passively.

## Phase 4: Referral & Partnership Engine (Weeks 4–12)
*Goal: Build a pipeline that compounds over time*

### 4.1 — Strategic Partnerships

These are your highest-leverage relationships. One good partner can send you 5+ clients per year.

**HR and benefits consultants:** They serve the same mid-size companies you want. They don't do travel. Introduce yourself and offer a referral commission or reciprocal referral relationship.

**Corporate event planners and DMCs:** They plan the event but often don't handle air. You handle air. This is a natural handoff. Reach out to 10 event planning companies in your area and offer to be their air travel partner.

**Corporate relocation companies:** They move employees but often don't book the travel. Another natural partnership.

**Business insurance brokers:** They serve mid-size businesses and are always looking for value-adds to offer clients.

**Approach:** Don't pitch. Ask for coffee or a Zoom call. Say: "I'm building a corporate travel consultancy and I think there's a natural overlap between what you do and what we do. I'd love to learn more about your business and see if there's a way we could refer to each other."

### 4.2 — Ask for Referrals (Once You Have Clients)

After you've delivered great service to your first client, ask: "Who else do you know that's dealing with the same travel headaches you had before you found us?" A warm intro from a happy client is 10x more effective than any cold email.

### 4.3 — Local Business Networking

Join your local chamber of commerce ($200–400/year, often within budget). Attend events. The room is full of mid-size business owners and operators who need travel managed. Also look for BNI chapters (Business Network International) — these are structured referral groups.

Outbound is where the ROI is right now.

## The One Thing That Matters Most

At this stage, the single most important activity is **having conversations with potential buyers.** Not perfecting the website, not writing blog posts, not designing business cards. Conversations. Every day, your job is to get into 1–2 new conversations with someone who manages corporate travel or plans events. Everything else — the site, the content, the SEO — exists to support that one goal.

If you do 15 cold emails and 10 LinkedIn outreach touches per day, 5 days a week, for 8 weeks — that's 600 cold emails and 400 LinkedIn touches. Conservative math says that generates 20–30 responses, 8–12 conversations, and 2–4 clients. That's your foundation.

The inbound engine (SEO, content, partnerships) takes 3–6 months to compound. Build it now, but don't wait for it. The outbound hustle gets you from zero to one. The inbound engine gets you from one to many.
